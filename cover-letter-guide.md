# Cover letter guide (Eddie Park)

**When to use:** only when Eddie explicitly asks for a cover letter (some roles require or invite one; most do not). Never part of the default application package.

**Output:** plain text, ready to paste into an application field. No letterhead or file unless Eddie asks. Target ~250-400 words, one screen.

---

## The idea

Write the cover letter like a piece of content that the reader would actually enjoy, not a form letter. It is a short chain of **hook -> retain -> reward** units bound by **open loops**, in Eddie's **quiet-confidence** voice. The pull comes from **specificity** - a real number, a real observation - never hype. It must **not** sound like a YouTube video: no "here's the crazy part," no manufactured urgency, no exclamation-stacking. Practitioner sharing findings, not a broadcaster.

## Structure: fit-reasons (the default and primary structure)

The reasons themselves change per role - that is the point. Pick them by mirroring the role's most important requirements.

**1. Opening hook (opens the macro loop)**
- Lead with the single sharpest, genuine signal or insight about the company (reuse the Step 3 signal), or a true "why I care about this company" when there is a real personal reason. Frame it about *them*, not Eddie's resume.
- A sharp market/category insight that proves he speaks B2B SaaS is one of the strongest openers - only when genuinely true.
- Then state the fit and open the loop: the role feels like a strong fit "for [N] reasons" (usually 3). Those N reasons are the macro loop the letter pays off.

**2. Body: N reasons, each its own hook -> retain -> reward unit**
- **Mini-hook:** name the requirement in *their* language (pain-mirror / relevancy), e.g. "This role needs someone comfortable with ambiguity and fast change."
- **Retain:** carry one open loop with a concrete proof or short story - situation -> challenge -> how -> result. Specifics, not adjectives.
- **Reward:** close the loop on the hard outcome/number (proof before principle): $103K / 15 B2B SaaS clients; +45.47% ROAS (RevPilots); 40% of deals inbound from content and 60% from email/LinkedIn outreach, the two synergizing; CRO end to end in GA4 / Hotjar / Convert.com; etc.
- **Overlap the seams** so the reasons read as one piece, not a disconnected list - seed the next reason as you close the current one.
- Keep each reason tight (2-4 sentences). Everything traces to the master resume - never invent.

**3. Close (rewards the macro loop + soft, mutual CTA)**
- Briefly pay off the opening: why this is a genuine fit, plus the long-game intent (building deep track record over the next 5+ years, lasting impact on team, company, and the customers the software serves) - drawn from the voice sample when it fits.
- CTA framed as **mutual and low-pressure**: "we should talk and learn more about each other" or "I'd love to talk and see if it's a fit." Never "please consider my application." Sell the conversation, not the ask.

## Voice guardrails
- Quiet confidence, kind competence; practitioner, not broadcaster. Specificity is the pull.
- No YouTube-isms, no hype, no urgency, minimal exclamation.
- Plain hyphens, never em-dashes.
- Honest: tell the pivot/growth story straight when relevant; never lead with the runway detail.
- ~250-400 words, plain text.
- Reuse the Step 3 signal and resume proof points; trace everything to the master resume.

## The YouTube framework, adapted (reference)
- **Hook** = a gap the reader cannot leave open. Open with the sharpest signal + the "N reasons" loop. Substance is the hook, not clever wording.
- **Retain** = never let every loop close at once. The "N reasons" is the macro loop; each reason opens and closes its own micro loop; overlap the seams.
- **Reward** = satisfy the promise. Each reason lands on a concrete proof. Proof before principle.
- **Register** = "but here's the thing," not "this will blow your mind."

## Voice samples (Eddie's own writing - match the register, do not copy verbatim)

**Sample A - fit-reasons / requirement-matching (this is the structure to follow):**

> 3 reasons:
> 1. This role requires "Entrepreneurial mindset - comfortable with ambiguity and fast changes.": I built my own B2B SaaS CRO agency Gro.X out of school, and hit 103K in revenue on my first year acquiring 15+ clients. I had no experience, and no existing "playbook" to rely on. I was able to achieve this through rapid testing of multiple channels and doubling down on what works using data.
> 2. You need someone crossfunctional that can speak the language of both sales and marketing (sales enablement, Sales SQL handoff, lead generation strategies involving both content and outreach, messaging impacting the sales pitch). About 40% of my customers for Gro.X came from inbound through content, and 60% through email and linkedin outreach - the two channels often synergizing. As I was the one doing both sales and marketing, I witnessed how content improves outreach reply rates and how leads contacted outbound can become inbound months later. I believe this translates well into the cross functional requirements of this role.
> 3. You need someone that's both analytical, creative, and can "drive growth experiments, optimize our funnels". I ran CRO and AB testing programs end to end for clients including project management in Airtable, design in Figma, AB development and set up in Convert.com. A google ads funnel I optimized for a client, Revpilot, improved ROAS by 45.47% and have improved conversion rates for multiple B2B SaaS websites.

**Sample B - narrative growth story (for voice/tone, and a "why this path / why in-house now" reason or close when the role invites it):**

> The project that I have most meaningfully grown as a person, is starting my business Gro.X.
> I am a growth marketer now. But I wasn't always.
> I started from a UX/product design background - graduated RISD with a design degree.
> However, I always had a desire to be closer to impacting business metrics.
> Sometimes I felt that design can be fuzzy to prove business impact.
> Then I discovered CRO - conversion rate optimization.
> I could design and run A/B tests which would show exactly how much my work impacted bottom line.
> In the very beginning, I could not find a role in CRO as I did not have prior experience or background in growth marketing.
> So I started taking on freelance clients. My first few clients came from my content on LinkedIn.
> After a while, my freelancing turned into my agency Gro.X.
> Over time, I was able to work with some amazing tech clients like Crayon, Bchex, Floworks (YC), Salesbricks, AR Workflow, SyncApps and many more.
> In my first year, I did lower 6 figures in revenue.
> But I still pivoted. Here's the thing - many b2b tech companies (startups and mid-market) dont have enough site conversions to warrant continuous AB testing.
> So many projects turned into short sprints or web design projects, not retainers. Which was an issue for retention (recurring revenue).
> I pivoted into Google Ads. And tried to serve B2B industries that were a more natural fit for Google Ads like accounting firms or commercial law firms.
> This was a mistake for two reasons. 1. I abandoned my original ICP, B2B SaaS companies, which I already had a growing audience around. 2. It was hard to significantly stand out among Google Ads agencies with 10+ years of track record, Google Ads being a very mature industry.
> Here's what I learned from this: Instead of replacing a service that was already getting clients, I should have added upsells after the initial CRO project for adjacent website related services like SEO.
> So I had to make a decision - either pivot back to CRO or go in-house at a B2B SaaS startup.
> I made the harder decision to join in-house at a startup I'm excited about, to build a deeper track record, skills, reputation, network, and audience for the next few years (5+).
> I am looking to play the long game and make a lasting impact on the teammates, company and the customers that the software serves.
