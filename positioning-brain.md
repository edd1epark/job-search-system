## 1. Positioning thesis (the spine)

He works at the intersection of analytical and creative: he builds customer-acquisition systems from scratch, tests channels fast, and doubles down on what the data proves. And because he built from zero, he is at home in startup chaos, where there is no playbook yet and someone has to build one.

**One-line compression:** a founder-operator marketer, analytical and creative, hands-on end to end with CRO, built to work where there's no playbook yet.

- Picks which bullets lead, and makes the path from designer to CRO to founder to in-house read as one arc.
- The test we hold every line to: could another growth marketer copy it onto their resume? If yes, cut it.

**How the founder meets the thesis:** not as a printed summary (we removed that), but through the "Founder & CEO" title, the systems-and-experiments bullets, the sales connector line, and the honest-journey note in outreach (section 6).

## 2. Read the company by growth stage (the core targeting model)

Companies hire for the pains of their stage, not one metric. Judge the stage first, then decide what to put in front. This is how we *find* good-fit roles, not just dress them up.

**Pre-seed / Seed (building 0 to 1):** little or no growth system yet. They need a builder who stands up early systems across several channels, experiments fast, and doubles down on what works. Foreground: Gro.X built from scratch, the automation systems, Thirdi (first 8 clients from zero), and the product / industrial-design 0-to-1 story (helped raise seed, launched real products). Design is an **asset** here. The "thrives where there's no playbook" trait is the headline.

**Series A / B (scaling what works):** they have a channel or two and need rigor and a pipeline sales can count on. They want someone who reliably turns traffic into inbound demos and tightens conversion. Foreground: CRO and experimentation, the content engine (40% of deals), funnel research. Background the heavier design roles.

**Series C+ / pre-IPO / post-IPO (specialize and scale):** defined lanes, efficiency focus. They want a specialist who owns one motion deeply. Foreground: the single lane that matches the job. Background the agency-owner and design angles.

## 3. Finding fit, then foregrounding the right evidence

Find roles you already fit, then put the matching parts of the story up front. Same career, different foreground. **An experience flips between asset and liability by context:** industrial design is noise for a performance-marketing role, but a selling point for an early-stage founder (seed-raise help, early product launches).

Read each role and judge.

- **Founding / early-stage (0 to 1):** Put up front: bootstrapped Gro.X solo; built the whole engine; 0-to-1 launches and seed-raise help; thrives with no playbook. Keep light: nothing, range is the point. Intro flavor: "Stands up the whole engine from zero."
- **Growth / CRO / Experimentation:** Put up front: end-to-end CRO on Convert.com; +45.47% ROAS; demo and landing conversion. Keep light: content volume, design roles. Intro flavor: "Installs an experimentation program inside the company."
- **Product Marketing:** Put up front: messaging for 15 SaaS clients; objections-to-copy; designer's eye. Keep light: outbound counts. Intro flavor: "Connects product, message, and conversion."
- **Content / Founder-led:** Put up front: LinkedIn engine (40% of deals, 4,800 audience); YouTube-to-shorts system. Keep light: A/B mechanics. Intro flavor: "Content that compounds into pipeline."
- **Performance / SEO / Lifecycle:** Put up front: Google Ads funnels; programmatic SEO on Webflow; funnel research. Keep light: design as a one-line edge. Intro flavor: "Fluent across the full funnel."
- **AI / Growth Engineering:** Put up front: Clay + Claygent, Apify, agents like Manus on GitHub knowledge bases + APIs. Keep light: design background. Intro flavor: "Builds AI marketing systems, not just uses them."

**Draw the connection explicitly.** When an experience is not an obvious fit, say why it matters for this role in one line, instead of making the founder connect the dots. Example (the cold-email bullet): even outside a sales role, that experience helps with sales coordination, SQL definition, and enablement. This is how the resume reads like content written for them.

## 5. Signal-based outreach formula (for the outreach engine, later phase)

Message = **[specific genuine compliment] + [a signal or insight you found] + [a small useful offer] + [low-pressure ask].**

Signals to mine: a feature they shipped, their ICP, a traffic or ranking trend, a weak spot on their demo or landing page, a content gap, the open role itself.

> "Hey [founder], love what you're building with [specific]. I noticed [signal]. I run conversion experiments for B2B SaaS and I'd happily do a short teardown of how to turn more of that traffic into demos. Saw your [role] opening too. Worth a quick chat?"

## 6. Honesty and objection handling

Eddie's instinct is right: being open about the pivot and what didn't work builds more trust with a founder than a polished claim. Founders live the same uncertainty, so an operator who can name a real failure and the lesson reads as credible. We use vulnerability as a strength, kept concise and forward-looking.

**The honest journey (concise, for outreach, cover notes, interviews, and the founder-facing resume top, not the ATS bullets):**
> "The CRO business was winning clients and delivering results, but I wanted more recurring revenue. The problem: about half of clients didn't have the traffic for valid A/B tests (so the work became web design, not retainers), and the rest usually got the big win in one 3-month sprint. So I pivoted Gro.X into Google Ads for traditional B2B firms (M&A, commercial law, accounting), leveraging my CRO background. It was the wrong bet: I left the B2B SaaS ICP and audience I had built, and couldn't stand out against Google Ads agencies with 10+ years of track record. In hindsight, I should have kept the CRO service that was already working and layered in adjacent upsells (SEO, chatbot builds) for recurring revenue. I debated restarting CRO, but made the harder call: take an in-house growth role at a startup, back in B2B SaaS, to build deeper track record and skills for the long run."

The founder should finish that and think: *he made a difficult decision and grew from it.* We stay honest about the journey and the lesson. We don't lead with the runway detail. The point is the growth and the deliberate choice of depth.

**Objections and how we defuse them:**
- *"Why close Gro.X? Did it fail?"* Use the honest-journey line. On the resume, Gro.X reads as a real company with real wins and awards.
- *"Will he leave to start another agency?"* The intro line ("over the next few years") and the depth narrative handle it.
- *"He's a designer, is he really a growth marketer?"* For non-design roles, keep the UX roles light and lead with growth, CRO, and systems. For early-stage roles, flip design into an asset.
- *"Agency owner is not an in-house teammate."* Show hands-on, end-to-end work and cross-functional coordination (devs, designers, SEO teams). The squad-leader role shows team operating.
