## 2. Read the company by growth stage

Companies hire for the pains of their stage. Judge the stage first, then decide what to put in front.

**Pre-seed / Seed (building 0 to 1):** little or no growth system yet. They need a builder who stands up early systems across several channels, experiments fast, and doubles down on what works. Foreground: Gro.X built from scratch, the automation systems, Thirdi (first 8 clients from zero), and the early-startup 0-to-1 story (Vandalsoft: helped raise seed, launched real products). Design and early-startup experience are an **asset** here. "Thrives where there's no playbook" is the headline.

**Series A / B (scaling what works):** they have a channel or two and need rigor and a pipeline sales can count on. Foreground: CRO and experimentation, the content engine (40% of deals), funnel research. Background the heavier design roles.

**Series C+ / pre-IPO / post-IPO (specialize and scale):** defined lanes, efficiency focus. Foreground: the single lane that matches the job. Background the agency-owner and design angles.

## 5. Signal-based outreach (guidelines first, template second)

The message must read as a candidate introducing himself, not a sales pitch. Eddie's LinkedIn still shows "Founder, Gro.X," so the default read is sales - defuse it by naming the role early. Never use "worth a quick chat?" or anything that sounds like booking a demo. Always use plain hyphens, never em dashes.

Hold every message to these:
- Open with a real, specific signal that builds rapport (genuine, never generic).
- A sharp market/category insight is one of the strongest openers - e.g., naming a real gap the company is positioned to win ("a gap in local-SMB data vs Apollo/ZoomInfo"). Proves he speaks B2B SaaS. Use only when true.
- Reflect the job post back ("I read the post and it felt like a strong fit: you want someone who builds in public, runs fast experiments, and is AI-native") - shows he actually read it and mirrors their language.
- Make it clear he's a candidate interested in the role.
- Low pressure, scannable for a busy founder, direct, respectful, human.
- Offer the conversion audit only when the role and company stage make it natural. Never criticize their product as an opener.

Opening-line formats (always anchored to a real signal):
- "Loved your take on {industry change}."
- "Loved that new feature you introduced to {product}, because {reason}."
- "Loved {thing} about your background - we have that in common."
- "Loved what {Company} is building, because {reason}."

Sample template (a starting point, not a cage):
> Hey {first_name},
> Love what you're building with {specific, genuine insight}.
> I saw you're hiring a {role} and wanted to introduce myself.
> {one situational line - a sharp insight, common ground, or the conversion-audit offer}
> Would love to hear where you're taking {Company}. Looking forward to your thoughts.

Worked example (Openmart Growth Lead):
> Hey Kathryn,
> Love what you're building - there's a real gap in local-SMB data vs traditional providers like Apollo/ZoomInfo, and Openmart seems strongly positioned to solve it.
> I saw you're hiring a Growth Lead and wanted to introduce myself. I previously ran a B2B SaaS CRO agency and basically lived in Clay and enrichment APIs building targeted lead lists, so I've felt the pain Openmart solves firsthand.
> I read the post and it felt like a strong fit - you want someone who builds in public, runs fast experiments, and is AI-native. Also noticed your Pinterest growth + A/B-testing background - feels like we'd speak the same language.
> Would love to hear where you're taking Openmart. Looking forward to your thoughts.

## 6. Honesty and objection handling

Being open about the pivot and what didn't work builds more trust with a founder than a polished claim. Use vulnerability as a strength, kept concise and forward-looking.

**The honest journey (for outreach, cover notes, interviews; not the ATS bullets):** the CRO business was winning clients but Eddie wanted more recurring revenue; about half of clients lacked the traffic for valid A/B tests, and the rest got the big win in one sprint; he pivoted into Google Ads for traditional B2B firms, which was the wrong bet (left his ICP/audience, couldn't stand out vs 10+ year agencies); in hindsight he should have kept CRO and layered upsells; he made the harder call to go in-house in B2B SaaS to build depth. The founder should finish that and think: he made a difficult decision and grew from it. Don't lead with the runway detail.

**Objections:**
- *"Why close Gro.X? Did it fail?"* Use the honest-journey line; Gro.X reads as a real company with real wins and awards.
- *"Will he leave to start another agency?"* The intro line ("over the next few years") and the depth narrative handle it.
- *"He's a designer, is he really a growth marketer?"* For non-design roles, keep UX light and lead with growth/CRO/systems. For early-stage roles, flip design and early-startup work into an asset.
- *"Agency owner is not an in-house teammate."* Show hands-on, end-to-end work and cross-functional coordination.
